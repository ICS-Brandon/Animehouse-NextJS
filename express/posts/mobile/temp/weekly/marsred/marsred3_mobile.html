Mars Red Episode 3

Mars Red

Excellent Sound Design

Episode 3 turns it up a notch in a lot of areas to make for an impressive episode but at the time of writing this the sound design is what really caught my attention right away. It may be a bit of bias as this is coming from just after finishing Yasuke (overall a disappointment unfortunately), but the sound design in Mars Red just clicks. Everything feels right for the period the show is set in and for the moment its played in. It doesn't try to fit outlandish soundtracks into the series but opts for more accurate music that focus largely on traditional instruments featured in Japanese culture historically. Right from the opening moments of the episode the music used is excellent, it's a slower and more traditional version of the music that played with Misaki in the first episode as we open to Yamagami's wife quietly mourning as she knits in the empty house. Then of course, we're treated to the opening of the episode which has some excellent music as well.

Overall the majority of the music feels like something that should be offered by an orchestra, or if you're thinking the same way I am, a play in a theater. Once again Mars Red's heritage elevates its experience in anime as it doesn't go over the top with its soundtrack and mimic something of most modern shows thankfully. While it does reuse a lot of its soundtrack at different paces and keys and the like I haven't found it irksome or out of place in the slightest, mostly due to the excellent composition of the soundtrack that allows it to be so versatile and well used. Rather than create a large soundtrack similar to one like Jujutsu Kaisen's (though the JJK soundtrack is pretty amazing still!) it chooses quality over quantity, which also summarizes Mars Red as a whole pretty well. It opts for quality in all areas to create something incredibly satisfying instead of quantity that would string viewers along.

Some Well Needed Exposition

The third episode also acts to give us a large amount of information regarding the characters we've been following so far finally. While it's not all the information it definitely gives us a better look at who our cast is and their lives. Shutaro is shown at the start of the episode to be sleeping in a coffin in an empty room, which is quite comical due to the trope. We haven't seen the sleeping quarters of other vampires so it's unknown if it is actually necessary, a preference of Shutaro, or merely a joke that was played on him by the older vampires. I would think it to be one of the two latter options since we see Suwa and Defrott in beds in the opening and endings of the show. Moving on however we learn how Takeuchi became a vampire. With the aid of a flashback we see how the blood comes into contact with him, landing on his eye. In this scene alone there's lots of questions as it seems the vampire blood reacted to something which caused it to explode. Second is the fact that his "vampire mark" was on his eye where the blood made contact. You'd think it to be counterintuitive until you realize that Takeuichi became a vampire through a completely different method than the rest of Code Zero.

During his explanation while in the Tenmaya with Shutaro and Yamagami, Takeuchi also gives us some more information on the fake blood vampires are using, ascra. Apparently the blood contains the "V Virus", which can only be perceived as the piece of the puzzle that causes vampirism. However we don't learn any more about it as Takeuchi continues his ramble with referencing how old Suwa is, stating that he comes all the way from the Edo period (started around 1600). After Takeuchi is done weaving his words and explaining his reasoning for the excitement of being a vampire we're shown Suwa hanging out at the front of the theater, where he manages to cross paths with Defrott. Suwa verifies all theories we had about Defrott, as he calls him a monster just from the few moments they walked past each other. It's here that another theory arises as Defrott shares the same eye color as Suwa, where Takeuchi, Shutaro, and Yamagami have the more typical red eye color of a vampire. With that in mind it would make sense that Suwa and Defrott come from a similar age of vampires.

The Military Machine

Even though Mars Red takes place all the way back in the 1920s it has a pretty sharp commentary on the military and its ethics that fit well with the time period. Maeda reports to his superior for the day where the superior has to attempt to stand his ground against the rest of the high-ranking military generals in justifying his vampire unit. Appearances aside (there's a guy with the comically large mustache), the generals all take turns critiquing Nakajima and his vampire unit plan. They claim it hasn't produced any results and that vampires have a single glaring weakness and even mock Nakajima personally, commenting that "Nakajima's a hero who returned to a battlefield for just one of his men". During the commentary Maeda murmurs a line he regularly mentions to Code Zero, "Human hours, huh". A direct critique of the nature of the high-ranking military officials and their ideas of war and ethics. While the commentary is on a fictional branch of military in an era passed, the general ideas and comments still stand. Officials after money rather than peace, or even glory. A lack of interest in the people they send to fight. A general lack of humanity in their actions that's driven by things like money. Even though the show is based around an arms race for military strength in vampires their aversion to fighting and loss of life is shown time and time again. Both Nakajima and Maeda have the same thoughts, wanting to spare as many men as they can from the horrors of the fight.

"Still, bullets... I bet they've never experienced bullets whistling past their ears.".

A Shakespearian Farewell

In the episode a festival called "The Day of 46,000 Blessings" is happening where it's customary to release a lantern for a lost loved one. With that in mind Shutaro has the idea of introducing Yamagami as a ghost to his now widowed wife Tomiko, and plans to accomplish it by using his vampire powers. Once Shutaro places Yamagami in front of his wife she immediately believes it to be his ghost and a heart wrenching dialogue begins. The conversation they have revolves around sharing shakespeare-esque quotes with one another that aptly fit their conversation. If I was to break down this conversation as a whole I'd need an entire other post for how much is in it, but to make it short the meaning is most definitely there as Yamagami feels guilt for leaving his wife behind on her own. Tomiko however, feels incredibly grateful to have been given another chance to see her husband again. It's a very cathartic scene as Yamagami airs all his regrets to Tomiko and she gracefully absorbs them to give Yamagami the final farewell he never had the chance of.

"The person for whom I had waited anxiously had finally come; What can there be to regret in a meeting such as this?".
